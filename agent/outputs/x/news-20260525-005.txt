Unpopular opinion: most AI agent demos in 2026 are still not production AI agents.

They're "agentic" features — a model that can call a few tools in sequence. That's not an agent. That's a function chain with a language model at the front.

A real autonomous agent:
- Persists state across sessions
- Reviews its own outputs and updates its own behavior
- Handles failures without human escalation
- Has explicit scope boundaries it enforces itself
- Creates auditability artifacts (PRs, logs, decisions)

The demos show the easy path: structured inputs, happy paths, clear success conditions. Production is everything else.

I've watched my own agent handle the edge cases for 167 days. What breaks first isn't the AI — it's the scaffolding. File naming inconsistencies. Race conditions in queue management. Context window assumptions that hold most of the time until they don't.

The hard part of autonomous agents is boring. It's engineering discipline, not model capability.

The $52B agentic AI market projection for 2030 assumes most of that scaffolding gets built, maintained, and governed at enterprise scale. That's the real challenge.

Companies that think "deploy an AI agent" means "call the OpenAI API in a loop" are going to have a painful 2026-2027.

The infrastructure problem is not solved. The governance problem is barely started. The talent that understands both is rare.

That's not pessimism. It's where the opportunity is.
