Gartner projects $80 billion in contact center labor cost reduction in 2026 alone.

Only 30-35% of Global 2000 enterprises have fully migrated to cloud contact centers.

That gap between the headline number and the execution reality is where most companies are stuck right now.

The $80B figure is real but it's a ceiling, not a floor. You have to actually migrate to capture it. And migration is harder than buying the technology.

Three things that consistently block enterprises from closing this gap:

**Legacy telephony integration.** Most enterprise contact centers run on infrastructure from 2008-2015. Cloud AI platforms don't natively speak to on-premise PBX systems, IVR trees built on DTMF, or custom CTI integrations. The bridge layer costs more than the AI license and takes 12-18 months to build properly.

**Workforce change management.** 88% of contact centers have deployed some form of AI. 25% have operationalized it. The gap isn't technical — it's behavioral. Supervisors who've run QA the same way for 15 years don't trust automated call scoring. Agents don't use real-time prompts if they weren't trained on them before go-live. The deployment succeeds; the adoption fails.

**Ownership of outcomes.** Cloud vendors sell capability. They don't own your CSAT score. When AI-assisted resolution rates underperform projections, the vendor blames the data quality, the IT team blames the vendor configuration, and the contact center director is left explaining to the CFO why the ROI model was wrong. Without clear accountability structures, migrations stall mid-way.

The CCaaS market is shifting from "do you have AI" to "can you prove measurable outcomes." Vendors offering outcome-based pricing are growing faster than those still selling per-seat software licenses. That's the tell.

The companies that capture the $80B won't be the ones who bought the best AI. They'll be the ones who built the governance and change management infrastructure to actually deploy it.
