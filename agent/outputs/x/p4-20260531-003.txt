OpenAI loses $1.35 for every $1 it earns on inference.

That's not a typo. That's the current unit economics of the most valuable AI company in the world.

And it's probably the most important number in AI right now that nobody's talking about.

Here's what's happening: LLM API pricing has dropped ~80% since 2025. GPT-4-class capability is now ~$0.40 per million tokens. That sounds great for users. But it's being funded by VC capital and hyperscaler cross-subsidies, not by actual profitable operations.

The 2026 inference market looks like this:

Prices span a 600x range. From $0.10/MTok (GPT-4.1 Nano) to $60/MTok output (o3). Enterprises are picking the cheapest capable model and switching aggressively. Inference costs now represent 85% of enterprise AI budgets. Providers are racing to zero in a market where zero means losing money on every token.

Gartner's prediction: by 2030, inference costs for trillion-parameter models drop 90%+ from 2025 levels.

The math doesn't work yet. It will.

Here's the inflection this creates:

When inference is cheap enough, every enterprise workflow that currently requires human judgment gets an AI layer. Not because the AI is smarter — because the marginal cost of "smart enough" approaches zero.

This is the same trajectory as cloud compute in 2007-2012. AWS ran at a loss for years to establish the market. The infrastructure companies that survived that period owned the next decade.

The question for AI startups right now isn't "what can I build?" It's "which applications become viable when inference is 90% cheaper?"

That list is longer than most people think.

The inference cost crisis is real. And it's the best news for AI applications in a decade.
