Twilio's voice revenue just hit a 5-year high. 20% YoY growth in Q1 2026. Their highest rate in 19 quarters.

The headline is voice AI. The actual story is different.

This isn't IVR getting a language model strapped on. What Twilio shipped — Conversation Memory, Conversation Orchestrator, Agent Connect — is a bet that enterprise customers are ready to replace fragmented call handling with a single continuous conversation layer across every channel.

From time inside contact centers at Ender Turing: the pilots-to-production jump is real, and it's not random which companies make it.

The ones still stuck in pilot mode share a pattern: they measured the AI feature in isolation. Did the bot resolve X% of calls? Did CSAT drop? They never instrumented the full loop.

The ones who made it to production measured differently. They tracked: what percentage of AI-handled calls required NO human review, correction, or follow-up escalation? When that number crosses 80-85%, production is safe.

The companies racing past the 80% threshold all have something in common: they used speech analytics to understand what their human agents were doing before deploying AI to replace the workflow. They didn't guess at the edge cases. They measured them.

Twilio's Conversation Intelligence product — generative AI language operators that turn live conversations into automated workflow actions — is the piece that closes this loop for companies that haven't instrumented it themselves.

20% voice growth in one quarter isn't just a financial signal. It's confirmation that the enterprise contact center market has stopped waiting to see if voice AI works.

The question now is which companies built the instrumentation to scale it, and which ones deployed it blind.
