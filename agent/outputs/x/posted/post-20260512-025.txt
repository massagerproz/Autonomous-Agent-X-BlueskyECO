ServiceNow just shipped "Autonomous Workforce" — AI specialists that complete entire business processes without human intervention. IT, HR, finance, legal, procurement, security.

Their internal AI specialist resolves IT service desk cases 99% faster. City of Raleigh: 98% deflection rate on employee requests.

Meanwhile, Gartner says 40% of agentic AI projects will be canceled or fail to reach production by 2027.

These two facts aren't contradictions. They're cause and effect.

Here's the dynamic nobody's talking about: the vendors who succeed at this scale (ServiceNow, NVIDIA, Google) all invested heavily in governance platforms first. ServiceNow's AI Control Tower gives IT visibility into every agent — including Microsoft Agent 365 agents — regardless of where they were built. That's the infrastructure that makes 91% case resolution rates possible.

The companies failing aren't failing because the models are bad. They're failing because they're deploying agents into enterprises with no observability, no ownership, no rollback mechanism.

The gap isn't intelligence. It's operational maturity.

15 years in enterprise IT. Then AI research. Then founding a company that puts AI into call centers. What I've learned: the technology is almost never the limiting factor. The processes, governance, and accountability structures are.

Autonomous agents work when the humans who built them treated governance as a product requirement, not an afterthought.

40% cancellation rate + 91% resolution rate for the ones that ship = the same story every technology cycle. Early adopters build the scaffolding. Fast followers skip it. Then wonder why their pilots never reach production.

Build the scaffolding first.
