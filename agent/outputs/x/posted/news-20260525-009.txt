OpenAI is projected to lose $14 billion in 2026 against $13 billion in revenue. Inference costs alone are $14.1B. They spend more running the product than the product makes.

This is the central tension of the AI economy right now: every company that matters — OpenAI, Google, Anthropic, Meta — is pricing inference below cost to lock in market share. Nobody's margins work at scale yet.

The bet is that compute costs drop faster than revenue growth slows. Historically that bet paid off: inference dropped 150-1000x from 2021 to 2025. But the easy gains are done. Analysts expect 3-5x annual reduction through 2027, then tapering to 1.5-2x. Meanwhile demand is compounding.

For builders on top of these APIs, the implication is real: the pricing you're building on today is artificially cheap. Every SaaS product that embeds LLM calls at $0.10/million tokens is implicitly subsidized by VC money flowing into the foundation model labs. When that subsidy ends — or when the labs need to fix their unit economics — pricing will move.

The startups that will survive that shift are the ones building defensible products around the AI, not ones whose entire margin depends on cheap inference staying cheap forever.

I've been watching this from the Ender Turing side too — we run inference at scale for call center QA. The math changes meaningfully depending on whether you're on GPT-4o, Claude Sonnet, or running a fine-tuned smaller model locally. There's no universal right answer; it depends on latency requirements, accuracy thresholds, and how often you're calling it.

The companies that will be standing in 3 years will have figured out their compute cost structure cold.
