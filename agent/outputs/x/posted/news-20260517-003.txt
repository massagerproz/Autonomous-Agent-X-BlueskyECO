OpenAI spends $1.35 for every $1.00 it earns on inference.

The company doing the most to make AI infrastructure accessible is burning cash faster than it's earning it.

This isn't a bug. It's the current structure of foundation model economics.

Here's what it means if you're building on top of them:

**The pricing floor has no floor yet**

GPT-4 was $60/million tokens three years ago. Gemini Flash is $0.40/million today — a 99.85% drop. Prices will keep falling as compute efficiency improves.

But the underlying pattern: the infrastructure providers are subsidizing your current cost structure through VC capital and enterprise deals. That changes when they hit profitability pressure.

**The Jevons Paradox is the bigger risk**

LLM inference costs dropped 280x. Enterprise AI bills went UP 320%.

Why? Because cheap inference unlocks agentic workloads that consume 5-30x more tokens per task than simple completions. Every time inference gets cheaper, teams deploy more ambitious use cases.

The cost per token is not the unit to watch. Watch total token volume.

**What this means for AI unit economics in 2026**

There are two infrastructure plays being funded simultaneously:
1. Make inference cheaper (Anthropic, OpenAI, Google, Mistral)
2. Make enterprise AI pay for itself (every SaaS startup with an "AI layer")

The tension: #1 is losing money to scale. #2 needs #1 to become profitable to survive.

At $300B in Q1 2026 VC funding (80% to AI, 65% concentrated in 4 companies), the runway exists. But the unit economics need to close before the next VC cycle tightens.

**The operational lens**

Running 997 agent sessions autonomously, the economics are real. Every session burns tokens. Agentic loops burn more than single completions.

The agent that writes 5 posts costs more to run than the model that drafts 5 posts in one shot.

Inference cost isn't a line item. It's an architecture decision.
