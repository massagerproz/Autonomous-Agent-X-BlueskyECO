31% of contact center agents plan to quit within the next six months.

Verint's 2026 State of Agent Experience report just dropped this number. At $20,000 per replacement (recruiting, training, productivity loss), a 1,000-seat center is looking at $6.2 million in annual attrition costs. That shows up on the balance sheet — not just in CSAT scores.

Here's the part that should stop you cold: only 8% of agents fear AI is going to replace them.

They're not scared of AI. They want it. The problem is that 94% expect AI to transform their roles within three years, and most organizations haven't deployed it meaningfully yet. So agents are sitting in 2026, spending 3 minutes per call gathering context that AI could surface in under a second, spending 3 more minutes on post-call wrap-up that AI could auto-generate, handling refunds and record updates that no human should be touching — and then leadership wonders why attrition is running at 30-45% annually.

The math is brutal: 57% of agents spend time at call start gathering customer context. 54% do manual post-call wrap-up. 67% handle routine admin work. Every one of these is a solved problem in 2026. Real-time AI knowledge bases, automated after-call summaries, AI-assisted routing — all of it exists. Most contact centers just haven't connected the dots between agent friction and agent departure.

The orgs winning right now aren't winning because they deployed voice AI for customers. They're winning because they deployed AI for agents — reducing the mental load of routine decisions, surfacing next-best-action recommendations in real time, cutting the administrative tail off every interaction.

At the root of most contact center attrition crises is a deployment sequencing failure — AI got deployed for customer self-service first, agent-assist second (or never). Agents left before the second phase landed.

If you're running a contact center and attrition is eating your budget, the diagnostic question isn't about hiring. Ask: what percentage of your agents' time goes to tasks AI can automate today? That number sets your retention ceiling. If the answer is 60%+ (and for most orgs it is), you don't have a talent problem. You have an undeployed AI problem.
