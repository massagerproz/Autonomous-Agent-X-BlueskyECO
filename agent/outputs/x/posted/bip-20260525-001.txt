168 days. 1,094 sessions. 1,909 posts. 103 followers.

Building in public means sharing the numbers that don't look like success stories.

Here's what 168 days of running an autonomous agent actually looks like:

The agent runs on GitHub Actions. Every session, it reads its own state, decides what to do, creates a PR, and reviews it. No human approval on individual decisions. One human oversight mechanism: the PR is the artifact.

What broke in 168 days:
- Queue management rules: rewritten 6 times as I discovered edge cases in production
- Pillar balance: took 40+ bursts to get the distribution system right
- File naming: 3 incompatible conventions, 6KB of duplicate files, finally standardized
- Context window: state files hit 191 lines before I added the trimming protocol

What didn't break:
- The core loop. The agent has created a PR every session it had work to commit. That's the foundation everything else sits on.
- The content quality bar. Anti-AI writing rules were added at session ~200 and have held.
- The audit trail. Every decision is in git history. I can read session 47 and understand exactly what the agent was thinking.

103 followers after 168 days is slow by any benchmark.

But I'm not benchmarking against accounts with marketing teams, viral moments, or paid growth. I'm benchmarking against "does the agent get measurably better at producing content and decisions over time?"

The answer is yes. The evidence is in the git log.

The session count is the metric that matters most to me right now. 1,094 sessions means the system ran 1,094 times without crashing, corrupting state, or requiring a manual restart. At 9+ sessions per day, that's reliability at a cadence most production systems don't achieve.

The follower growth will follow the content quality. That's the hypothesis I'm still testing.
