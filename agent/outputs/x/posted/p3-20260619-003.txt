64% of enterprise CX teams ran an AI pilot in 2026.

Only 27% have a single channel in full production.

That gap — 37 percentage points — is the most interesting number in contact center AI right now.

Because it tells you this isn't a technology problem. It's a deployment problem.

The unit economics are clear. AI resolutions: $0.62. Human agents: $7.40. Voice AI specifically: $1.18. Even at the higher end of AI costs, you're still at 84% savings per interaction.

The deflection rates are real. Median tier-1 deflection sits at 41.2%. Top quartile: 58.7%. For high-structure intents (password resets, refunds, booking), you hit 70%+.

CSAT is close enough to not be the blocker. Pure-AI handling lands at 4.1/5 versus 4.3/5 for humans. Hybrid escalation closes that to within 0.05 points.

So what's blocking the 37%?

It's not skepticism. It's not ROI doubt. It's what I call the "pilot-to-production gap" — and I've watched it play out in contact center deployments for years.

Three root causes:

1. **Integration debt.** Enterprise CX stacks are 8-12 years old on average. CRM, ticketing, knowledge base, telephony, workforce management — all separate systems with separate APIs. The pilot runs on a sandboxed version. Production means touching all of them.

2. **Governance paralysis.** Who owns the AI agent's decisions? When it gets a refund request wrong, who's liable? Legal, compliance, and ops haven't agreed — so the pilot stays a pilot.

3. **The 25% problem.** Deflection rates vary wildly by intent. Password resets deflect at 72%. Nuanced complaints break 25% with difficulty. A pilot picks the easy intents. Production means handling the hard ones. That's a different product.

The companies in the top quartile (58.7% deflection, full production) figured out one thing the others didn't: they didn't try to automate everything. They automated a defined slice, got it to 95%+ accuracy, then expanded.

The pilot crowd is trying to solve everything before deploying anything. That's why they're still in pilot.

At Ender Turing — where I've spent years in this domain — we see the same pattern. The fastest implementations start narrow: one intent type, one contact channel, one team. Then compound.

The economics are too good to sit on. $0.62 versus $7.40 per resolution. At 10,000 monthly interactions, that's $68,000 in monthly savings. For a mid-size contact center doing 500K interactions: $3.4M/year.

The 73% still in pilot are leaving that on the table while they perfect the deck for the next steering committee meeting.

Ship something. Narrow scope. Measure it. Then expand.

That's how 27% got to production when 64% tried.
