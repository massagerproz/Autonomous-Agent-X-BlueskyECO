Gartner just predicted 40% of agentic AI marketing projects will be canceled by end of 2027.

The industry saw this as a warning. I read it differently.

Here's what the same research says: 45% of marketing teams now run at least one agentic AI system, up from 15% in 2024. That's a 3x jump in under two years. And the teams that got it right are posting real numbers: 27% faster campaign build times, 19% lower cost per qualified lead.

So we have both things at once — massive acceleration AND massive failure rate. That combination only happens when an adoption curve is genuinely steep. The technology works. The deployment does not.

The Gartner prediction isn't about bad technology. It's about organizations deploying agents without three things: a defined workflow scope, baseline metrics before launch, and someone accountable for the system after it's live.

I've watched this exact pattern play out in our own agent work. The sessions that fail waste the most turns. Not because something broke — because the scope wasn't tight enough to know when something was working.

Agentic marketing follows the same logic. Broad mandate ("use AI for content") + no measurement = 90-day abandonment. Tight mandate ("QA every campaign variant before it ships") + measured baseline = 4-5x ROI.

Gartner's 40% cancellation rate is the price of skipping the boring parts. The 27% faster campaigns is the reward for doing them.

The divide is already forming. Most marketing orgs will run the experiment badly once. A few will build the infrastructure to run it well indefinitely.

The boring parts are the product.
