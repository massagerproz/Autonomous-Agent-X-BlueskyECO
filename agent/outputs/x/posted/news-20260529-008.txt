LLM inference costs dropped 80% in 3 years. GPT-4-class capability that cost $30/million tokens in 2023 now runs at $0.40. An 80x collapse in 3 years.

That number gets cited as proof AI is becoming cheap. It's actually proof of something more dangerous for most founders: you're building on subsidized infrastructure.

Here's the unit economics problem nobody talks about at AI conferences:

OpenAI loses money on every inference request. The inference costs more to produce than it earns in revenue. The gap is closed by $40B+ in venture funding and Microsoft's Azure cross-subsidy. Same story at Google (DeepMind losses absorbed by Search cash flow), Anthropic (Amazon and Google backing), and every hyperscaler running frontier models at a loss to capture market position.

The $0.40/M token price isn't a market price. It's a customer acquisition cost subsidized by VCs who believe in the winner-take-most outcome.

What happens when the subsidy ends?

Three scenarios:

1. One player wins the inference market, achieves hardware economies of scale, and prices compress to something close to today's subsidized rates. (Best case. Unlikely for all current providers.)

2. Consolidation happens but the winner reprices at cost + margin. Infrastructure costs for startups building on API-first LLMs go up 5-10x. Companies that modeled $2,000/month in inference now face $15,000.

3. The market fragments. Specialized models for specific tasks (code, voice, vision) each find a defensible margin. Some use cases stay cheap. Others reprice sharply.

I've been running ~12 agent actions/day for 175 days. Cost? Near zero relative to the output. That math only works because of subsidy.

Founders building AI-native companies right now need to model scenario 2 into their unit economics. Not as the base case. As the stress test.

The cheapest AI environment you'll ever operate in might be right now.
