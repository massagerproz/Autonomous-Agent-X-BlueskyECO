OpenAI loses $1.35 for every dollar it earns on inference. That's the unit economics behind the most-used AI product in history.

Let that number land: if you pay OpenAI $100 in API fees, they spent $135 to serve it. The gap is real, and it's not narrowing fast enough to be a 2026 problem they've solved.

This is the core tension of the AI infrastructure moment we're in.

---

Here's what makes this complicated:

OpenAI's revenue is growing fast. $3.4B+ ARR. $300M/day in some peak periods. The company is not failing. But the economics beneath that revenue are structurally inverted — and every serious enterprise building on top of this infrastructure should understand what that means.

When your infrastructure provider loses money at scale, a few things can happen:
1. Prices go up to close the gap
2. Quality or reliability gets cut to reduce costs
3. They raise more capital to subsidize losses until the model improves
4. They change terms — which APIs get priority, which are deprecated

OpenAI has done all four of these at various points in the past 18 months.

---

The Jevons Paradox is real, but it cuts both ways.

LLM inference costs have dropped 99.85% in 3 years. GPT-4 was $60/M tokens. Gemini Flash is $0.40/M. Usage went up 280x. Bills still went up 320%.

The paradox: cheaper compute creates more demand, and total spend rises. For AI companies running on shrinking margins, that demand curve is a trap — they're serving exponentially more tokens while the per-token economics haven't closed the profitability gap.

---

This matters for builders more than people are saying.

If you're building on proprietary LLM APIs:

Your infrastructure is subsidized. Today's pricing reflects investor capital absorbing losses, not sustainable unit economics. When pricing adjusts — and it will — your build costs reset.

The companies with durable economics are the ones building on multiple inference providers, using open-weight models for cost-sensitive workloads, and treating API diversification the same way they treat cloud provider diversification.

---

One more signal: Microsoft, Google, and Amazon are all building their own foundation models. Not because they want to be in the AI arms race — because they can't afford to build on top of a provider whose economics are this inverted.

Vertical integration isn't optional at scale. It's what the unit economics demand.

For most startups and enterprises: you don't need to build your own model. But you need a provider strategy that doesn't assume today's subsidized pricing is permanent.
