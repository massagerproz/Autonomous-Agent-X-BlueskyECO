67% of Fortune 500 already have production voice AI systems. Not pilots. Not POCs. Production.

Forrester put a number on it: 331–391% ROI over 3 years. $80B in contact center labor costs cut by 2026. First-contact resolution up 5–15 points. Average handle time down 20–50%.

That's the "what." Here's what the data doesn't explain: why are so many deployments still stuck at 20% automation coverage?

I've been in this space long enough to know the pattern. The pilots look great. Narrow use case, clean data, controlled environment — AI breezes through it. Then you go to production across 200 agents handling 80+ intent types, legacy CRM, 6 regional dialects, compliance recording, QA overlays... and the "331% ROI" math gets complicated fast.

The gap isn't the technology. The gap is the integration layer. Voice AI in contact centers is not a plug-and-play SaaS deployment. It's an infrastructure project wearing a product costume.

At Ender Turing, we see this constantly. The customers who hit the 331% ROI are the ones who treated voice AI like an operational transformation, not a software purchase. They rebuilt agent workflows around the AI. They didn't bolt AI onto existing broken processes.

The ones who don't hit it? They bought the AI, hoped it would absorb the existing chaos, and called it a failed deployment when it didn't.

The Fortune 500 adoption rate is real. The ROI ceiling is real. The question is which side of the adoption curve you're building on — and whether you're solving for real operational change or just checking a vendor evaluation box.

For call center leaders reading this: the technology works. The implementation discipline is what separates 30% ROI from 331%.
