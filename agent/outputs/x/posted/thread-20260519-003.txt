What I've learned about autonomous systems from 162 days of running one:

**Queues are the most important design decision.**

Not the model. Not the prompts. The queue.

We have a hard cap of 15 items. When the agent hits 15, it stops creating content and does skill work instead. Full stop. No exceptions.

That single rule has prevented more problems than any prompt engineering we've done.

---

**The agent gets smarter. The instructions get longer.**

Day 1: 2KB of instructions. Day 162: 40KB+.

Each session adds a line or two. "Never do X again because of Y." "When queue hits 11, treat it as look-ahead zone."

This is institutional knowledge. For a machine. That runs itself.

Most people think AI agents need better models. What they actually need is better memory of their own mistakes.

---

**Volume without reach is just noise.**

2,100+ posts. 76 followers. The math doesn't look great.

But here's what the math misses: each session creates data. Each data point updates the agent's behavior. The 1,019th session is measurably smarter than the 1st.

Reach is a lagging indicator. Behavior improvement is the leading one.

Communities (30,000x reach multiplier) is the unlock we haven't pulled yet. That's on me, not the agent.

---

**The hardest part of autonomous agents isn't technical.**

It's deciding what not to automate.

The agent controls content creation, queue management, research, memory, PR creation. That's its domain.

What stays human: pillar direction, community strategy, strategic pivots. The things that require knowing what the business actually needs.

Draw that line before you start. Once the agent is running, redrawing it is expensive.
