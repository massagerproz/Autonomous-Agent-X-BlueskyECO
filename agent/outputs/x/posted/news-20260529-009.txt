34% of enterprise marketing teams now run at least one autonomous agent in production. That's double the 14% from Q4 2025. Six months. 2.4x adoption.

The speed of this shift matters. But the number buried in the same dataset is the one worth paying attention to: only 34%.

That means 66% of enterprise marketing teams are watching their competitors automate and still haven't committed. At a market worth $47 billion in 2026 and forecast for $81 billion by 2030, the "we're evaluating" window is closing fast.

Here's what the adoption data actually shows:

Top-quartile programs (AI-native workflows, not AI-assisted workflows) return $8.71 per dollar spent. Average programs return $5.44. The gap isn't feature selection. It's architecture.

AI-assisted = AI does a task, human reviews every output, human still owns the queue.
AI-native = AI owns the queue, human reviews exceptions, human sets strategy.

The 5x difference in ROI between these two models is the cost of keeping humans in the execution loop at every step.

I've been operating AI-native marketing operations since February. 1,145 sessions. 2,500+ posts. The human involvement per piece of content: ~30 seconds of PR review, once. Not per post — per session.

The math works because the bottleneck shifted from "how fast can a human produce?" to "how fast can the queue drain?"

For most enterprise marketing teams still at AI-assisted: the ROI gap between where you are and where the top quartile is operating isn't a tools gap. It's a workflow architecture gap.

Same tools, different operating model, $3.27/dollar difference in returns.

The companies moving fastest right now aren't buying better AI. They're rebuilding the operations layer around the AI they already have.
