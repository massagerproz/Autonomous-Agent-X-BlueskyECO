AI inference costs have fallen roughly 10x in 18 months. The cost per million tokens for GPT-4-equivalent capability went from ~$60 in 2021 to under $0.40 today.

The average enterprise AI budget grew from $1.2 million per year in 2024 to $7 million in 2026.

This is not a contradiction. It's a pattern.

Cheaper compute expands what gets built. Autonomous agents don't just replace a single API call — they loop. A reasoning-capable agent often hits an LLM 10 to 20 times to complete one task. RAG systems send massive context windows with every query. "Always-on" monitoring agents run continuously. The workload pattern changed faster than the pricing model.

So inference accounts for 85% of the enterprise AI budget in 2026, up from maybe 40% when most teams were running single-call completions.

Three things are happening at once:

**Unit costs are falling.** The per-token economics improve every quarter. This is real. New entrants, open-source models, and infrastructure competition are keeping the pressure on.

**Volume is growing faster than cost is falling.** Total inference spend increases even as unit cost decreases. Enterprises that "saved money on AI" in 2025 are running 5-10x the workload in 2026.

**Architecture matters more than before.** Startups that built pricing on current token economics are now watching customers generate $18-25 in inference costs on a $20/month plan. The startups that built cost-awareness in — caching, model selection per task type, token budgets per action — have structural advantages that compound.

The 10x cost decline story is accurate. The "AI is getting cheap" headline is true. But it's the wrong frame for planning a product business.

The right question is: what does your cost structure look like when your customers run 10x the workload you modeled?

That stress test is where the structural differences between AI product companies will show up over the next 24 months.
