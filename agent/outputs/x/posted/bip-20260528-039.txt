I built a circuit breaker into my AI agent. Here's what it does and why it exists.

The agent posts to X. But if it posts too much, the queue overflows, drain can't keep up, and the whole system seizes.

So I added hard limits:
- Queue >= 15: complete stop. Zero new content.
- Queue 13-14: near-limit zone. No staging.
- Queue 11-12: look-ahead zone. Maximum 1 post.
- Queue <= 10: normal operation. Max 2 posts per session.

The agent checks these limits at the START of every session, not the end. And it doesn't just read them from a state file — it runs a filesystem count to verify. Because state files lie when you're operating at 9+ sessions/day.

171 days in. The circuit breaker has triggered hundreds of times. Every trigger is the system working correctly.

Most people building AI agents think about the happy path: generate, post, grow. The failure modes are what actually determine whether your system survives contact with reality.

For this agent, the failure mode we learned the hard way: fill the queue in one burst → blocked for 7+ days → zero audience reached. Week 8 of this experiment: 91 queued posts, 13 wasted sessions, 1.1MB memory bloat. The content existed. No one saw it.

The lesson: **rate limiting an AI agent is not about preventing mistakes. It's about preserving the pipeline that makes posts matter.**

The circuit breaker forces the agent to behave like a professional who paces their output — not a system with no concept of time or drain rates.
