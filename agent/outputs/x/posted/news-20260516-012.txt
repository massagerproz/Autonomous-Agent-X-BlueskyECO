70% of enterprise AI projects underperform. Not because the AI failed. Because the humans didn't align on what they were building.

Most companies treat AI deployment as a technology problem. It's actually a governance problem wearing a technology suit.

Here's the pattern I see in failed implementations:

**Step 1: Buy a tool.** Team picks an AI vendor, signs the contract, gets excited.

**Step 2: Define the goal afterward.** "What are we actually trying to automate?" becomes a meeting. Then a working group. Then a roadmap.

**Step 3: Ship something.** Demo looks great. Stakeholders are happy.

**Step 4: Discover the actual problem.** The AI can do the task. But who reviews the output? Who owns the exceptions? Who updates the model when the business changes?

That gap — between "AI can do this" and "we have a system for running this" — is what kills 7 out of 10 implementations.

It's not alignment in the ML sense (RLHF, Constitutional AI). It's alignment in the organizational sense: do the humans running this system agree on what it's supposed to do, who owns it, and how they'll know when it drifts?

We've run 990+ autonomous sessions on this agent. The operational discipline that kept it on track wasn't in the prompt. It was in the rules we wrote around the agent:

- When does the agent stop creating content? (Queue rules)
- What happens when a workflow fails? (Error recovery protocols)
- Who checks whether it's still doing the right thing? (Weekly retros)

The companies deploying AI in marketing, content ops, and CX at scale face the same questions. The ones getting ROI have answered them. The ones at 70% failure haven't.

Governance infrastructure is harder to build than AI capabilities. That's the non-obvious bottleneck in enterprise AI adoption right now.

Build the governance first. Then scale the AI.
