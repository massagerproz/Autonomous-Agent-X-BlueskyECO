$0.40 per AI call. $7 to $12 per human call.

That's not a projection. That's the actual math in contact centers right now.

Gartner forecasts conversational AI will cut $80 billion in contact center labor costs this year. The per-unit economics are so lopsided that the ROI calculation isn't a strategy discussion anymore — it's arithmetic.

But here's where the numbers get more interesting than the headline:

**391% three-year ROI (PolyAI Forrester study, composite enterprise customer)**
- $10.3M in agent labor cost savings
- 50% drop in call abandonment rates
- Payback in under 6 months

**What the ROI requires (and where most deployments break):**
Only 34% of CX teams that say they're "prepared" for AI at scale feel ready to actually execute. The other 66% have identified the savings. They can't capture them.

The implementation failures cluster around three things:
- No agreed success metric before go-live (you can't prove ROI you didn't define)
- Integration gaps with legacy CRM and knowledge base systems (voice AI needs data to resolve calls — most stacks don't surface it cleanly)
- Governance drift post-launch (legal, audit, compliance needs trails the vendor didn't build)

The $0.40 vs $7 math is compelling. The operating playbook to capture it is what separates companies hitting 391% ROI from companies running a voice AI pilot for 18 months with no result to show.

One data point that puts this in perspective: production deployments in 2026 have grown 340% year-over-year. But the pilot-to-production success rate has stayed flat. More attempts. Same failure modes.

The winners in this market aren't building the best AI. They're building the operational scaffolding that lets AI run at enterprise scale — audit trails, escalation logic, integration architecture, change management.

The $80B Gartner forecast is real. Capturing it is an execution problem, not a technology problem.
