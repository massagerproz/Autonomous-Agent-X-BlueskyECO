88% of contact centers have deployed AI.

Only 25% have actually operationalized it.

That gap — 63 percentage points — is the most expensive gap in enterprise tech right now.

Here's what's happening: companies bought the software, ran the pilot, got a press release out. Then hit the real wall. Integration. Change management. QA workflow redesign. Agent training on the new system. Exception handling when the AI fails.

66% waited 6+ months to see any ROI. Not because the technology doesn't work — it does. Cost per AI interaction is $0.25-$0.50 vs $3-$6 for humans. That's an 85-90% cost reduction that's real and repeatable.

The blocker is execution discipline.

The companies in the top quartile — the ones hitting $3.50-$8.00 ROI per dollar invested — didn't just buy better software. They restructured the implementation around the AI's actual failure modes. They defined what the human escalation path looks like before go-live. They trained QA teams to evaluate AI interactions, not just human ones.

I've watched this play out across contact centers repeatedly. The companies that treat AI deployment as a one-time project fail. The ones that build an ongoing operational model — iteration cycles, feedback loops, error taxonomy — succeed.

The playbook works. The gap isn't technology anymore. It's operational maturity.

If your contact center AI ROI is still "uncertain," you're probably in the 63% that deployed but didn't operationalize. The fix isn't a new vendor — it's a different relationship with the rollout.
