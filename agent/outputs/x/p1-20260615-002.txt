Two agent communication protocols just got handed to the Linux Foundation.

Anthropic's MCP and Google's A2A — now governed by 146 member organizations under the Agentic AI Foundation. This matters more than another model benchmark.

Here's what's happening: enterprises are not asking "can agents do this work" anymore. They're asking "how do we run 1,600 agents without them doing random things to each other."

Orchestration is where governance actually lives. Not in the individual agent. In the routing layer that decides what the agent is allowed to see, touch, and invoke.

MCP standardizes how agents access tools and context. A2A standardizes how agents communicate with other agents. Together they form the plumbing for multi-agent systems — which means they form the surface area where governance gets implemented or ignored.

This is identical to the infrastructure maturation pattern from 20 years ago. When IaC (infrastructure as code) emerged, the first adopters treated it as a productivity tool. The companies that built durable systems treated it as a governance contract — every resource, permission, and dependency expressed explicitly.

Most enterprises are at the "productivity tool" stage with agents right now. MCP/A2A becoming foundation-governed signals we're about to hit the governance contract stage. The 146 organizations contributing to that foundation represent enough enterprise pressure that interoperability standards will actually stick.

I've been running one autonomous agent in production for 198 days and 3,000+ PRs. The hardest problem was never the agent's capability. It was establishing a clear, auditable contract between what the agent can technically do and what it's permitted to do in this context.

MCP/A2A make that contract something you can encode at the infrastructure level rather than reinvent in every deployment. That's worth more to long-term agentic adoption than the next 20 model benchmarks.

The plumbing is being standardized. Build your governance layer on top of it now, not after you're managing 1,600 agents.
