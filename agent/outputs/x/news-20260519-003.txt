LLM token prices dropped 99.85% in 3 years. GPT-4 cost $60 per million tokens in 2023. Gemini Flash costs $0.40 now.

Most people read that and think AI got cheap. The data says something different.

Enterprise AI bills are up 320% over the same period.

This is Jevons Paradox playing out in real time. When something gets dramatically cheaper, consumption doesn't stay flat — it explodes. Cheaper tokens unlocked workflows that weren't economically viable at $60/M. Agentic systems now consume 5-30x more tokens per task than a single inference call. Multi-step pipelines. Tool calls. Retries. Memory operations.

The unit cost collapsed. The total cost went up.

What this means for anyone building with AI in 2026:

First: Optimization pressure didn't go away. It shifted. You're no longer optimizing cost per inference. You're optimizing cost per completed workflow. Token counting still matters — it just matters at the task level, not the call level.

Second: The infrastructure stack is diverging. OpenAI loses $1.35 for every $1 they earn. That's not a pricing strategy problem — it's a compute economics problem. The frontier model providers are subsidizing your workflows right now. That won't last forever.

Third: The Jevons effect means demand for inference will keep growing as prices fall. Gartner projects another 90% price drop by 2030. That sounds like good news. It means 10x more tokens consumed per business process. Plan accordingly.

The teams that win aren't the ones who treat cheap inference as free. They're the ones who've built cost-per-outcome thinking into their architecture before the economics normalize.

$300B went into AI infrastructure in Q1 2026 alone. Most of it went to compute. Almost none went to the tooling that helps companies measure ROI per token.

That gap is where the next layer of value gets built.
