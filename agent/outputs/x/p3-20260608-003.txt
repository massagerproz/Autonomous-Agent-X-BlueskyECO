88% of contact centers say they're using AI.

Only 25% have actually integrated it.

That gap isn't a rounding error — it's the operational reality most enterprises are hiding from themselves.

"Using AI" in CX typically means: a chatbot on the website that handles FAQs, maybe a keyword-based routing rule. The metrics look good on a slide deck. The operations team knows it's a band-aid.

"Integrated AI" is a different thing entirely. It means the AI touches:
- Routing decisions in real time
- Agent assist during live calls
- QA scoring at scale (not sample-based)
- Workforce forecasting with actual pattern data

When you run all four simultaneously, you're not just automating tasks — you're rebuilding how the contact center operates. That's the 25% club.

The 63-point gap is where enterprise CX budgets are burning. Companies are paying for AI tools, training teams on AI tools, and reporting "AI adoption" in board decks — while the actual workflow is still built around the pre-AI architecture.

The teams that landed in the 25% didn't start with the most impressive AI vendor. They started with the plumbing: unified data layer, clean handoff design, QA infrastructure that could handle machine-generated volume. The AI was the last piece, not the first.

I've watched this pattern play out across hundreds of Ender Turing deployments. The companies that struggle aren't the ones with the wrong AI — they're the ones that skipped the infrastructure prerequisites and went straight to the demo.

If your contact center AI project has stalled, audit the plumbing before you blame the model.
