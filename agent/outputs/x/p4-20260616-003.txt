AT&T quietly moved its automated customer support off GPT-4 and onto a fleet of fine-tuned Mistral and Phi models earlier this year.

Result: 90% reduction in monthly API costs. 70% improvement in response speed.

Nobody talks about this because it's not a launch announcement. It's just good engineering.

The monolithic LLM era is over in enterprise. Not because frontier models got worse — they got better. But because "better" turns out not to be the thing enterprise AI actually needs for 80-90% of workloads.

Here's what the math looks like at scale:

GPT-4 class inference: $2-30 per million tokens.
SLM inference (7B parameter, fine-tuned): $0.10-0.50 per million tokens.

That's a 10-30x cost difference. For high-volume, domain-specific tasks — customer support, document extraction, classification, intent detection — the SLM wins on cost AND latency. The frontier model adds overhead without adding value.

Gartner projects enterprises will use task-specific models 3x more than general LLMs by 2027. That timeline feels slow to me. AT&T isn't an early adopter — they moved because the economics are obvious.

The architecture that's emerging: routers. Route routine tasks to fine-tuned SLMs. Reserve frontier LLMs for complex reasoning where they actually earn the inference cost.

This is the same efficiency logic that killed mainframes. You don't run your entire operation on the most expensive compute when cheaper, faster, purpose-built options exist.

For AI infrastructure strategy, the question has shifted from "which LLM?" to "which tasks actually need a frontier model, and which can be handled by a fine-tuned SLM at 1/20th the cost?"

Most enterprises haven't asked that question yet. The ones that have are seeing 80-90% API cost reductions on routed workloads.

AT&T just published their answer. Others are running the same experiment quietly.
