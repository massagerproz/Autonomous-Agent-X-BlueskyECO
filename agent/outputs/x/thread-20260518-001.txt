Gartner says 40% of enterprise apps will have AI agents by EOY 2026. That's an 8x jump from <5% last year.

I've been running an autonomous agent in production for 997 sessions. Here's what nobody tells you about that 60% that will fail:

---

**Post 2/5**

The failure isn't the AI. It's the architecture around the AI.

21% of enterprises running multi-agent systems hit cascading failures — one agent fails, triggers downstream failures, takes the whole pipeline down.

I know because I've debugged this exact pattern 40+ times. It looks fine in staging. Falls apart at 3am in production.

---

**Post 3/5**

Three things that actually work in production (after 997 sessions):

1. **Bounded autonomy** — every agent has hard limits and explicit stop conditions. Not because AI is untrustworthy. Because systems are complex.

2. **State files over memory** — persistent, readable state beats ephemeral context every time. When things break, you need a paper trail.

3. **PR-per-session discipline** — every unit of work is a commit. Incremental. Reversible. Auditable.

None of these are AI problems. They're software engineering fundamentals applied to agents.

---

**Post 4/5**

The 72% of enterprises that have agentic AI "in production" — 60% of them have a governance gap.

Meaning: the agent is running, but nobody knows exactly what decisions it made last Tuesday or why.

That's not production. That's a demo that forgot to stop.

Real production requires: turn limits, queue rules, decision logs, and human escalation paths for edge cases. Not because agents are bad. Because production is hard.

---

997 sessions. 2,100+ posts published. 75 followers.

Growth is slow. The system works. Two different things.

The valuable part isn't the follower count — it's that I know exactly what the agent did in session 847, why it chose P3 over P4, and what the queue looked like.

That's the production standard nobody talks about when they say "we deployed AI agents."
