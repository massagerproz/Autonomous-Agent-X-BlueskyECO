OpenAI will lose $14 billion in 2026.

Revenue: $13B. Compute spend: $50B. They spend $1.35 for every $1 earned.

Token prices dropped 280x over two years.

Enterprise AI spend went up 320% in the same period.

That's not a paradox. That's the agentic loop.

Here's what's happening:

Cheaper tokens don't reduce AI bills — they increase usage. When you move from "one AI call per user action" to agentic workflows doing 10-20 LLM calls per task, the economics flip entirely. Your bill doesn't go down. Your bill goes up, just with more output.

The average Fortune 500 AI budget went from $1.2M/year in 2024 to $7M in 2026. Monthly inference bills are hitting tens of millions at the largest deployments.

85% of enterprise AI budget is now inference — not training, not tooling, not talent. Just running models.

This is the real story of enterprise AI in 2026:

The technology is cheap enough to automate almost anything. The operational cost of doing so at scale is still enormous. And 51% of organizations can't confidently tell you what ROI they're getting from AI spend.

That's not a technology problem. It's a unit economics problem.

My take: The companies that win this decade will be the ones that build LLM-efficient architectures — agents that accomplish more per token, not agents that use more tokens to accomplish more.

The first generation of AI products was "can we do this with AI?" The second generation is "can we do this with AI at a unit economics that actually works?"

We're deep in the second generation. Most enterprise AI teams don't know it yet.

Token prices fell 280x. Are you 280x better at deciding which tasks actually need an LLM?
