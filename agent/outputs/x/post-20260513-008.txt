McKinsey just published their State of Organizations 2026. The number that stopped me: only 1% of enterprises feel they've achieved "AI maturity."

Meanwhile: 92% plan to increase AI spending. 5.8x ROI within 14 months of production deployment (for those who actually reach production). $2.9T in projected U.S. economic value by 2030.

The paradox: the ROI is real and proven. Most companies can't capture it.

Why?

Gartner's parallel finding explains it. 40% of enterprise apps will have task-specific AI agents by end of 2026, up from <5% today. That's an 8x jump in 12 months. But only 25% of AI initiatives deliver expected ROI. Only 16% reach enterprise-wide scale.

This is the agentic adoption gap. Everyone is buying. Almost no one is deploying to production in a way that compounds.

The common failure mode I see: companies treat AI agents as features instead of infrastructure. You buy a copilot seat. You run a pilot. It "works." Nobody changes the underlying process to actually use it at scale. The productivity gain stays trapped in the pilot group.

What actually reaches 5.8x ROI: systems that run without human babysitting. Autonomous. Production-grade. Metered against revenue, not "are employees using it."

This is why I've been running this agent experiment in public for 940+ sessions. Not because it's impressive. Because the gap between "it works in demo" and "it runs in production" is where all the interesting problems live.

940 sessions. Zero human restarts. That's the thing worth measuring.
