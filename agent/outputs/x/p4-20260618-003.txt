Token prices have fallen 280x in two years.

Enterprise AI spend is up 320% in the same period.

That's not a typo. Costs per token collapse. Total bills explode. Welcome to the Jevons Paradox for AI infrastructure.

Here's the mechanism: agentic AI workflows require 5 to 30 times more tokens per task than a standard chatbot query. You're not just asking a question. You're running reasoning chains. Tool calls. Memory lookups. Verification loops. Each "agent step" burns context that a simple prompt never would.

The CFO approves AI because per-token costs are falling. Then the engineering team deploys an agent that routes 40 steps to complete a single customer support case. The unit economics that looked great on a whiteboard look nothing like the production invoice.

The companies getting this right are separating two very different calculations: inference cost per token (falling fast, great news) vs. inference cost per business outcome (entirely dependent on workflow design, often rising).

A customer support agent that resolves 1 ticket in 40 LLM calls costs 5-10x what an engineer assumed. Multiply by 10,000 tickets per day. That's the AI budget that surprises the board in Q3.

The gap between naive implementation and optimized deployment is 5-10x cost difference — enough to determine whether the unit economics work at all.

Three things that actually determine AI infrastructure economics in 2026:

1. Tokens per business outcome, not tokens per query. Count the full chain, including retries, validation, and context injection.

2. Context window discipline. Agents that bloat their context window on every call will cost 3-4x more than agents designed to compress and summarize. Memory architecture is cost architecture.

3. Model routing. Frontier models for reasoning. Smaller models for classification, formatting, extraction. Mixing tiers by task type cuts costs 60-80% without sacrificing quality where it matters.

The companies that will win on AI economics aren't the ones waiting for prices to fall further. They're the ones who already designed for cost efficiency when prices seemed fine.

The floor keeps dropping. The Jevons ceiling keeps rising. Build like both are true.
