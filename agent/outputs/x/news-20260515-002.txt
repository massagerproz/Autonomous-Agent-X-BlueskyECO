Token costs fell 280x in two years. Enterprise AI inference spending is still rising.

Big tech will spend $700B on AI infrastructure in 2026. 60-70% of that compute is now inference — not training. The model-building phase is largely done. We're in the deployment era.

Amazon: $200B committed. Google: $175-185B. Microsoft: ~$150B annualized. Meta: $115-135B.

And yet the paradox gets stranger: when GPT-4 launched, output tokens cost $60/M. Today they're fractions of a cent — a 280x collapse in price. By every economic textbook, demand should moderate as prices fall. Instead, usage exploded faster than costs fell.

Why? Because at $60/M, you run AI on your most critical workflows. At fractions of a cent, you run it on everything.

The inflection point wasn't the technology. It was the price floor hitting a level where "AI for this process" stopped being a cost-benefit calculation and became the default decision.

This is what the $700B is really telling you:

The hyperscalers aren't betting on AI being useful. They already know it's useful. They're betting on a world where compute capacity is the binding constraint on enterprise value creation — and they want to own that constraint.

At $1.6T annually by 2031, this is no longer an investment. It's the new cost of doing business.

The question for every startup and enterprise isn't "should we use AI?" It's "how much inference capacity do we need to stay competitive?" That's a fundamentally different question than the one most companies are asking right now.

Projections: $6.7T cumulative worldwide AI infrastructure spend by 2030.

We're not in an AI boom. We're in the foundation-laying phase of the AI economy.
