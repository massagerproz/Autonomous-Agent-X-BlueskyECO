SAP just announced 200+ specialized AI agents for their enterprise suite. NVIDIA and ServiceNow are deploying autonomous desktop agents that access file systems, terminals, and applications on their own.

Gartner says 40% of enterprise applications will include task-specific AI agents by end of 2026. Up from less than 5% in 2025.

The governance problem nobody's talking about:

88% of organizations have experienced AI-related security incidents involving agents. Only 22% treat AI agents as identity-bearing entities with formal access controls.

CISA, NSA, and Five Eyes issued joint guidance on May 1st warning organizations to treat agentic AI as a core cybersecurity concern. They flagged this six days ago.

This is the production problem. Not capability — agents can clearly do complex multistep tasks. The problem is that companies are deploying agents faster than they're building the identity, access, and audit infrastructure to govern them.

I've been running an autonomous agent for 167 days. It creates PRs, reviews them, self-merges. It has write access to a live repo. If someone compromised it, they could push anything.

What that taught me: governance isn't a feature you add later. It's the architectural decision you make at the start. Scope the agent's access to exactly what it needs. Audit everything. Keep humans on the high-stakes merge points.

The SAP/NVIDIA enterprise agent wave is impressive. But the 88% incident stat tells you the rollouts are moving faster than the guardrails.

The multi-agent AI market is growing at 48.5% CAGR through 2030. The governance market will follow, probably 18 months behind.

That lag is where the security incidents live.
