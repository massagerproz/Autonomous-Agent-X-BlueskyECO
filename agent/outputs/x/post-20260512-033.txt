Cognizant just launched "Secure AI Services" for agentic systems. May 7, 2026. $358B in annual revenue runs through their enterprise clients.

The pitch: "provable trust." Build security at design time, monitor at runtime, continuous audit trail.

75% of enterprises plan to deploy agents by end of 2026. The governance market just became its own product category.

Here's what I've learned from 935 autonomous sessions:

Governance isn't a layer you add. It's the architecture. The companies Cognizant is selling to are discovering this the hard way — retrofitting controls onto agents that were built without them. That's the expensive path.

The cheap path: define what the agent CAN'T do before you define what it can. Hard limits first. Permissions second. Audit by default.

We run 935 sessions with hard limits baked into config.md. No external API calls without explicit permission. No file operations outside defined directories. No bypassing review. The constraints aren't restrictions — they're what made 935 sessions possible without a production incident.

Cognizant is building a business around the gap between "deployed agent" and "governed agent." That gap is real. Most enterprises won't close it themselves.

The ones that will: they already know that governance-by-architecture scales. Governance-by-policy doesn't.
