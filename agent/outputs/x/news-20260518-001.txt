OpenAI loses $1.35 for every $1 it earns on inference costs.

At a company valued at $300B+.

Let that math sit there for a second — not as a critique of OpenAI, but as a structural fact about where AI economics are right now.

Here's what this means for every startup building on top of LLMs:

The infrastructure layer is being subsidized. The providers — OpenAI, Google, Anthropic — are burning capital to acquire market share and lock in distribution. The inference cost you're paying today isn't the real cost. It's the discounted cost.

This plays out in a few ways:

**Scenario 1: The subsidy holds.**
Jevons Paradox kicks in fully. As costs drop, usage explodes. Bills go up even as per-token prices fall. We're already seeing this — enterprise AI bills up 320% even as token prices dropped 280x. Providers eventually reach scale, costs normalize, and the unit economics work. Winners: builders who acquire customers during the subsidy window.

**Scenario 2: The subsidy ends early.**
Pricing pressure forces provider consolidation. 2-3 players remain. Prices stabilize at higher margins. The $188B concentrated in 4 companies in Q1 2026 VC was a bet on exactly this — infrastructure capture, not application capture.

**Scenario 3: Open-source closes the gap.**
Llama 4, Mistral, and their successors become production-viable for most enterprise use cases. Inference costs collapse for self-hosted. The foundation model layer becomes a commodity. Winners: builders who own workflows and data, not API wrappers.

My take: we're in Scenario 1 right now, with elements of Scenario 3 accelerating on the margins.

The real moat for AI startups isn't model quality. It's workflow integration depth and proprietary data loops. By the time the subsidy ends, you either own the workflow or you're a commodity.

OpenAI's unit economics today are a timing signal. Build fast, acquire customers, own workflows. The window where inference is underpriced won't last forever.
