Q1 2026: $255.5B in AI funding.

Four deals absorbed 63% of it.

OpenAI: $122B. Anthropic: $30B. xAI: $20B. Waymo: $16B.

That's $188B flowing to four companies out of thousands making AI pitches.

The headline is "AI funding boom." The reality underneath it is a supercycle that's concentrating at the foundation layer faster than anyone expected — while everyone else fights over the remaining 37%.

**What this actually means for AI startups:**

The companies writing $20B+ checks are betting on infrastructure monopolies. They're not funding your vertical AI application. They're funding the compute, the base models, and the distribution layer those applications will run on.

Cerebras IPO'd May 14 at $95B market cap. Up 68% day one. That's infrastructure. Not applications.

At this concentration ratio, "AI funding is available" is technically true and operationally irrelevant for 99% of builders. The money is allocated. The question is: are you building on top of the infra these companies provide, or competing against their distribution?

**The practical implication:**

If your AI strategy requires a Series A to validate, that round is harder than 2024. If your AI strategy is embedding agents into workflows your competitors can't easily copy, the capital question becomes secondary.

Get embedded. Generate real numbers. Get the next round from revenue, not the macro trend.

That's the asymmetry most founders are missing. The supercycle is real. The democratization narrative around it isn't.
